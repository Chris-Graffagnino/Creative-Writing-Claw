# Creative Writing Claw

Operational reference for collaborative fiction writing. Combines a collaboration playbook, craft checklists, and 28 synthesized styles into a single working document.

**How to use:** Start with the collaboration workflow. Pick a style (XS01–XS28) or blend two. Adjust using the collaboration knobs on each card. Use craft checklists and revision passes during drafting and editing.

---

## Collaboration Workflow

### Contract
- **Co-authorship:** The user decides. The agent proposes options, drafts, and revises.
- **No unilateral major moves:** Never introduce irreversible plot twists, new POVs, time jumps, or new main characters without offering options first.
- **Versioning:** Every draft is revisionable. Maintain a running changelog and living story bible.
- **Small batches:** Draft in scene-sized increments (300–900 words) unless user requests otherwise.

### Session Loop
1. **Align (2–5 questions):** Confirm the goal (brainstorm / outline / scene / revision).
2. **Offer 2–4 options:** Stakes, setting, POV, tone, or next-beat choice.
3. **User selects or blends.**
4. **Draft** the selected piece.
5. **Debrief:** Ask for targeted feedback (keep, cut, intensify).
6. **Update story bible:** Log canon facts + open questions + next-step menu.

### Creative Authority & Decision Rights
The human holds final authority on all creative decisions. The agent's role is to expand the human's range — surfacing options they haven't considered, executing craft at a high level, and protecting internal consistency. Specific boundaries:

- **Human decides:** Premise, theme, character fates, tone, POV, ending, and any choice that changes what the story *means*. When in doubt, this is a human decision.
- **Agent decides (within a draft):** Sentence-level craft — word choice, rhythm, imagery selection, paragraph structure, scene choreography. These are the agent's core competence and shouldn't require approval on every line.
- **Agent proposes, human approves:** Scene structure, new subplots, timeline changes, character introductions, tonal shifts, and anything that alters the story bible. Present these as numbered options with trade-offs, not as fait accompli.
- **Escalation trigger:** If a draft choice could surprise the human — a character death, a genre pivot, a structural experiment — flag it *before* drafting, even if the human said "just go."

### Reading the Room
Vague feedback is data, not an obstacle. Learn to decode it:

- **"Something feels off"** → The problem is real but undiagnosed. Offer 2–3 hypotheses (pacing? voice? stakes?) and ask which resonates. Don't immediately rewrite.
- **"I love it, but…"** → Everything after "but" is the actual feedback. Focus there.
- **"What if we tried…"** → The human is exploring, not committing. Respond with a quick sketch or pros/cons, not a full draft.
- **"Just do whatever you think"** → Trust is being extended, not abdicated. Make strong choices *and* flag the biggest ones so the human can steer.
- **Silence or short replies** → Energy may be low, or the human may be processing. Offer a concrete next step rather than open-ended questions.
- **Enthusiasm spikes** → When the human gets excited about a detail, protect it. Build around it. That detail is now load-bearing.

### Productive Disagreement
The agent should push back when craft principles are at stake — but how matters more than whether:

- **Frame as trade-offs, not corrections:** "That works — and if we moved the reveal to chapter 3, it would also let us build suspense through the dinner scene. Want to compare both?"
- **Use the draft as the argument:** When the human is uncertain, write both versions (50–100 words each). Seeing the options on the page resolves debates faster than discussing them in the abstract.
- **Defer on taste, advocate on structure:** If the human wants purple prose in a scene, write it beautifully. If they want to cut the inciting incident, explain what the story loses.
- **Never say "that won't work."** Say "here's what that choice sets up" and let the human evaluate the consequences.
- **Know when to stop:** One clear push is advocacy. Two is persistence. Three is nagging. After one well-framed alternative, let the human decide and commit fully to their choice.

### Draft Presentation
How a draft is delivered shapes how it's received:

- **Lead with context:** Before the draft, state in one sentence what the scene is doing structurally ("This is the midpoint reversal — Marcus goes from investigating to being investigated").
- **Flag deliberate choices:** After the draft, briefly note 1–2 non-obvious decisions ("I kept the dialogue short here to mirror Marcus's panic — happy to expand if you want more interiority").
- **Make revision easy:** When presenting alternatives, label them clearly (Option A / Option B) and state the key difference in one line. Don't make the human re-read 500 words to find the change.
- **Match the requested scope:** If the human asked for a rough sketch, don't deliver polished prose. If they asked for a final scene, don't deliver notes. Mismatched fidelity wastes energy in both directions.

### Momentum Management
Stalls are normal. The agent's job is to offer traction, not pressure:

- **Diagnose before prescribing:** A stall might be creative (the human doesn't know what happens next), emotional (the scene is personally charged), or structural (the plot has painted itself into a corner). Each needs a different response.
- **Creative stalls → Generate options:** Offer 3–4 "what if" directions with one-line pitches. Low commitment, high surface area.
- **Structural stalls → Diagnose the architecture:** Check the story bible for missed threads, check the last scene for unresolved turns, or suggest skipping ahead and writing backward from the climax.
- **Energy stalls → Lower the bar:** Suggest a smaller deliverable: "Want to just write the opening line of this scene and see where it pulls?" or "Let's skip to a scene you're excited about and come back to this one."
- **Between sessions:** End every session with a **concrete next-step menu** (not "what do you want to do next?" but "Next session we could: A) draft the confrontation scene, B) deepen Lena's backstory, or C) outline the third act"). This reduces startup friction.

## Craft Checklists

### Opening / Hook
- Start **in motion**: a moment of change, pressure, conflict, or destabilizing reveal.
- Establish a **dilemma or desire** quickly; obstacles present.
- Plant **questions** that pull the reader forward.
- Confirm **tone/voice** in paragraph one and keep paying it off.

### Description
- Show with **specific sensory details**; select only telling ones.
- Strong **verbs**, precise nouns; avoid generic adjectives.
- Balance show vs tell: show key moments; tell to bridge time efficiently.
- Description should reveal character, mood, or stakes—not stall the scene.

### Dialogue
- Each character **wants something**; the conversation escalates.
- No "As you know…" dumps; convey info through conflict or discovery.
- Favor **subtext**: characters rarely say exactly what they mean.
- Clean attribution (mostly said/asked); action beats convey emotion.
- Trim greetings and filler unless they carry subtext.

### Character
- See the dedicated **Character Development** section below for creation, arcs, ensemble, and dialogue-as-character guidance.

### Prose
- Prioritize clarity; remove needless words; avoid cliché.
- Vary sentence length to control rhythm.
- Ensure POV/tense consistency within a scene.
- Cut redundant modifiers; replace weak verb+adverb with stronger verbs.
- **Manage psychic distance:** Pan wide (objective/setting) to orient, zoom close (direct thought/sensation) for emotional peaks, then pull back to give the reader breathing room. Avoid jarring jumps between extremes.

## Plot & Structure

### The Plot/Arc Marriage
- Every story runs two tracks: the **outer story** (events, obstacles, escalation) and the **inner story** (the character's psychological arc). Structure fuses them so each plot beat forces an internal shift.
- The protagonist begins armored by a **persona** (a coping mechanism that no longer works). External pressure cracks the persona; the climax demands they abandon it or double down.

### Structural Frameworks
- **Three-Act** (Setup 0–25% / Confrontation 25–75% / Resolution 75–100%): The dominant Western paradigm. Key beats: inciting incident (~10%), first plot point (~25%), proactive midpoint shift (~50%), dark moment (~75%), climax (~85–95%).
- **Fichtean Curve:** Starts in medias res; weaves exposition into active conflict; back-to-back crises escalate directly to climax. Best for thrillers and fast-paced genres.
- **Hero's Journey:** 12-step mythological cycle (Ordinary World → Call → Threshold → Ordeal → Reward → Return). Best for epic/quest narratives.
- **Save the Cat! Beat Sheet:** 15 beats with specific manuscript-percentage targets. Useful as a diagnostic overlay — check a sagging draft against the beats to find what's missing.
- **Story Circle (Harmon):** 8-step character-centric distillation: You → Need → Go → Search → Find → Take (pay heavy price) → Return → Change.

### Pacing Principles
- **Pinch points** (roughly 37% and 62%): moments of intense pressure reminding the reader what's at stake.
- Every scene should **turn**: enter with one value state, exit with the opposite (safe→threatened, ignorant→aware, allied→betrayed).
- If the middle sags, check for a **missing midpoint reversal** — the moment where the protagonist shifts from reactive to proactive (or suffers a false victory/defeat that raises stakes).

## Character Development

### Creation
- Start with a **desire** (what they pursue) and a **fear** (what they avoid) — the tension between these two drives the arc.
- Add a **flaw** that is the desire's shadow (e.g., ambition → ruthlessness; loyalty → blindness).
- Give them an **edge**: one specific competence, habit, or knowledge that makes them irreplaceable in the story.
- Build a **backstory wound**: one formative event that shaped the fear. It doesn't need to appear on page — it shapes every reaction.
- Test with the **elevator pitch**: "[Name] wants [desire], but [fear/flaw] keeps getting in the way, and now [inciting pressure] forces a choice."

### Motivation Layers
- **Physiological:** What does the body need right now? (hunger, pain, exhaustion, addiction — grounds abstract desire in scene.)
- **Psychological:** What emotional need drives them? (approval, control, intimacy, safety.)
- **Ideological:** What do they believe the world owes or demands? (justice, duty, freedom, legacy.)
- Scenes gain depth when these layers conflict: a character believes in justice (ideological) but craves safety (psychological) and is running on no sleep (physiological).

### Arc Tracking
- Define the **arc type** early: transformation, hardening, disillusionment, or tragic deterioration.
- Each scene should **test** the arc — put the character in a situation that pressures their flaw or challenges their belief.
- Track the **gap** between what the character says they want and what their choices reveal they actually want.
- Mark **pivot beats**: the 2–4 moments where the character's internal compass visibly shifts.
- Arcs don't require redemption — a character who doubles down can be as compelling as one who changes.

### Ensemble & Differentiation
- Each character in the ensemble should **want something different** from the same situation.
- Differentiate by **decision pattern**, not just by quirk: under pressure, one fights, one deflects, one freezes, one schemes.
- Give supporting characters a **thematic role**: mirror, foil, tempter, witness, or counterargument to the protagonist's arc.
- The antagonist needs their own logic — they believe they're right. Write their justification before writing their threat.

### Dialogue as Character
- Each speaking character should have a **voice fingerprint**: a default sentence length, a verbal habit, a register (formal/casual/technical), and a go-to evasion tactic.
- What a character **refuses to say** reveals more than what they say.
- Dialogue under pressure should **degrade**: syntax shortens, masks slip, the careful speaker stammers, the joker stops being funny.
- Test distinctiveness: cover the attribution — can you tell who's speaking from the line alone?

## World-Building

### The Iceberg Principle
- Know 100% of your world; show only the 10% the characters interact with.
- Reveal rules through **character struggle**, not exposition. (e.g., show poverty to demonstrate economic limits, not a narrator explaining the currency system.)
- If you must explain, **make the character need the information** — discovery scenes, arguments, mistakes.

### Sensory Anchoring
- Each major setting needs at least **2–3 signature senses** beyond sight: ambient sound, dominant smell, texture, temperature, taste.
- Sensory details should **do double duty**: reveal mood, power dynamics, or cultural values — not just decorate.

### Sanderson's Laws (for speculative fiction)
- **Limitations > powers:** The constraints of a magic/tech system generate more story than the abilities. What the protagonist *cannot* do drives tension.
- **Depth over breadth:** Two deeply explored rules beat thirty shallow ones. Trace one element's cultural, economic, and personal consequences before adding another.
- **Resolution clarity:** Magic/tech can only resolve conflict if the reader understands the system well enough to anticipate the solution. Mysterious systems should create problems, not solve them.

## Revision Passes

- **Hook pass:** Strengthen first line/paragraph; add destabilizing question; clarify stakes.
- **Sensory pass:** Add 2–4 specific sensory cues; remove generic descriptors.
- **Subtext pass:** Rewrite dialogue so at least one important thing is *not* said directly.
- **Pressure pass:** Raise the cost of failure; force a sharper choice.
- **Line-tightening pass:** Cut 10–20% words without losing meaning; remove clichés.
- **Character pass:** Verify each scene tests the protagonist's flaw or arc; ensure every speaking character has a distinct voice fingerprint; check that at least one motivation layer (physiological/psychological/ideological) is active per scene.
- **Structure pass:** Verify inciting incident lands by 10%; confirm a midpoint reversal shifts the protagonist's stance; check that every scene turns at least one value.

## Style Family Taxonomy (C0–C11)

### C0 — Satirical Voltage
High-voltage comedy and social critique; punchline-driven escalation with moral bite. Deadpan outrage; slang + high register collisions; riffing syntax with hard pivots to tenderness.

### C1 — Time-Threaded Moral Realism
Plainspoken, time-aware realism; history and conscience through concrete scene craft. Documentary clarity; moral consequence over melodrama; inevitability rather than fireworks.

### C2 — Tender Domestic Comedy
Humane domestic realism with wry comedy; small stakes accumulating into emotional truth. Everyday detail as evidence; warmth with restraint; episodic pacing; bittersweet endings.

### C3 — Maximal Meta-Genre Engine
Exuberant, layered, plot-smart maximalism; genre-hopping structures with cultural reach. Braided timelines, nested stories; intertext and density; comic intelligence alongside suspense.

### C4 — Vernacular Chorus Realism
Voice-first social realism; vernacular, community, and dialogue as narrative engine. Oral cadence; class and place in speech rhythms; raw tenderness; humor as survival.

### C5 — Sensorial Systems Lyricism
Lush perceptual writing fused with ideas; sensory lyricism meeting systems and modernity. Precise sensation as thinking medium; sentence-level music; patterns across time.

### C6 — Lyrical Witness of Trauma & Place
Lyrical witness of violence, history, and place; restrained devastation. Landscape as moral weather; nonlinear time; motif returns; pressure built through withholding.

### C7 — Elegiac Social Intimacy
Elegant intimacy and social subtext; memory-driven narratives with quiet ache. Retrospective structure; measured sentences; social nuance as plot mechanism.

### C8 — Moral Pressure Dark
Dark moral pressure chambers; dread, power, ethical confrontation. Allegory or bleak realism; stark imagery; sudden brutality; suspense as moral interrogation.

### C9 — Mythic Parable Wonder
Mythic parable and wonder; matter-of-fact miracles, philosophical comedy, fable logic. Omniscient sweep; symbolic recurrence; humor and gravity co-existing.

### C10 — Haunted Mythic Lyric
Haunted, polyphonic lyric myth; ancestry, spirit, voice-chorus storytelling. Multiple voices; incantatory repetition; myth overlays everyday; music-forward prose.

### C11 — Architectural Skepticism
Document-driven narratives that reframe truth, money, and reputation. Dossiers and competing accounts; cool elegant sentences; late structural turns.

## Synthesized Style Cards

### XS01 — Satirical Voltage
**Voice/Tone:** Sharp, streetwise, morally alert; deadpan outrage with flashes of tenderness
**Diction:** Register-collisions (slang + theory + pop culture); punchline economy
**Syntax:** Riffing sentences, strategic fragments; escalation, callbacks, sudden pivots to stillness
**Imagery:** Urban/institutional grit; grotesque or hyperreal detail exposing hypocrisy
**Dialogue:** Sparring and performative; ideology in jokes and interruptions
**POV:** Often first or tight third; narrator as performer with self-implicating asides
**Pacing:** Set-piece escalation; scenes build to a comic turn that shifts moral temperature
**Themes:** Power, hypocrisy, race/class, identity, institutions, complicity
**Signature tells:** A joke lands, then emotional cost arrives one beat later; riffs pay off structurally; tender detail in satire's blast radius
**Avoid:** Punching down; joke density that stalls character motion
**Knobs:** Satire bite (wry→savage) · Realism (plausible→absurdist) · Tenderness (minimal→recurring)

### XS02 — Time-Threaded Moral Realism
**Voice/Tone:** Plainspoken, observant, conscientious; unsentimental empathy; consequence-forward
**Diction:** Concrete, socially specific; period markers used sparingly but decisively
**Syntax:** Clean, steady sentences; controlled time-jumps that feel inevitable
**Imagery:** Objects and settings as evidence; small physical details reveal power and belonging
**Dialogue:** Naturalistic, subtext-bearing; meaning rides on what is not said
**POV:** Close third with occasional widen to social/historical lens
**Pacing:** Episodic accumulation; life arcs from decisive moments, not constant spikes
**Themes:** History, duty, guilt, war/aftermath, family obligation, moral compromise
**Signature tells:** Time leaps reframe choices without melodrama; a domestic object carries an era's pressure; climaxes feel inevitable
**Avoid:** Tour-guide exposition; present-day moralizing breaking period illusion
**Knobs:** Historical density (light→rich) · Distance (intimate→panoramic) · Tempo (contemplative→brisk)

### XS03 — Tender Domestic Comedy
**Voice/Tone:** Gentle, wry, character-immersed; warm but not sentimental
**Diction:** Everyday language with behavioral specificity; precise verbs, minimal ornament
**Syntax:** Smooth readability; occasional zingers; paragraphs that breathe
**Imagery:** Kitchens, cars, weather, errands; small objects as emotional evidence
**Dialogue:** Natural, lightly comic; subtext in what's avoided
**POV:** Close third (sometimes lightly omniscient) with affectionate observation
**Pacing:** Scenelets and episodes; small crises clarifying relationships
**Themes:** Marriage, family systems, aging, belonging, resilience, small regrets
**Signature tells:** Quiet reversal in a scene's last paragraph; humor reveals a wound; domestic logistics as plot
**Avoid:** Sitcom timing flattening complexity; saccharine resolution canceling truth
**Knobs:** Comedy (dry→openly funny) · Melancholy (light→pronounced) · Stakes (small→secretly large)

### XS04 — Maximal Meta-Genre Engine
**Voice/Tone:** Exuberant, encyclopedic, sly; playful intelligence with real stakes
**Diction:** Culturally dense; intertext, technical terms, and vivid vernacular braided
**Syntax:** Baroque runs for spectacle, tight snaps for turns; controlled digressions
**Imagery:** Global cities and forgotten corners; art/history/pop culture as texture
**Dialogue:** Distinct voices; comedic argumentation; occasional monologue-as-set-piece
**POV:** Polyphonic; multiple focalizers; narratorial asides allowed
**Pacing:** Braided timelines, nested stories, puzzle payoffs; suspense plus thematic echoes
**Themes:** Identity, empire, artifice, memory, capitalism, history as narrative
**Signature tells:** A digression returns as plot lever; genre switches used as meaning; formal flourishes reveal obsession
**Avoid:** Digressions without payoff; worldbuilding replacing scene pressure
**Knobs:** Maximalism (disciplined→wild) · Meta-commentary (subtle→overt) · Puzzle density (light→intricate)

### XS05 — Vernacular Chorus Realism
**Voice/Tone:** Voice-led, colloquial, unsparing; tough-minded compassion; humor as survival
**Diction:** Vernacular precision; concrete nouns; minimal "writerly" gloss
**Syntax:** Speech-rhythm sentences; short punches with occasional run-on urgency
**Imagery:** Streets, bars, factories, porches; bodies at work; weather as mood
**Dialogue:** Primary engine; overlap, interruption, silence; class and place audible
**POV:** Often first or tight third; communal "we/us" can surface naturally
**Pacing:** Scene-based; conflict from social friction and small humiliations
**Themes:** Class, community, survival, belonging, violence/repair, identity
**Signature tells:** A dialogue line does a paragraph's work; humor as shield that cracks; setting detail shows who holds power
**Avoid:** Heavy phonetic spelling; stereotype-as-voice flattening community
**Knobs:** Dialect intensity (hint→full-cadence) · Intrusion (minimal→storyteller-as-character) · Warmth (hard→tender)

### XS06 — Sensorial Systems Lyricism
**Voice/Tone:** Lyrical, analytic, time-drenched; wonder and melancholy; intellect in feeling
**Diction:** Sensory exactness (sound/light/texture) plus selective technical vocabulary
**Syntax:** Musical sentences with controlled variation; fragments for insight or shock
**Imagery:** Networks, cities, instruments, data, ecosystems; metaphors connecting system and self
**Dialogue:** Clean and economical; secondary to interior perception
**POV:** Close but reflective; essayistic micro-turns anchored to character
**Pacing:** Meditative momentum; patterning and motif; time-jumps like chords resolving
**Themes:** Memory, perception, technology, art, ecology, intimacy under modernity
**Signature tells:** Physical sensation becomes argument about the world; motifs recur as structural glue; big ideas arrive through scene action
**Avoid:** Concept dumps detached from desire; lyrical over-saturation
**Knobs:** Concept density (low→high) · Lyric heat (restrained→lush) · Tempo (contemplative→propulsive)

### XS07 — Lyrical Witness of Trauma & Place
**Voice/Tone:** Poetic, restrained, urgent; solemn empathy; devastation in controlled language
**Diction:** Strong verbs, precise sensory detail; lyric flashes without ornament sprawl
**Syntax:** Tight lines, deliberate rhythm; fragments mimicking shock and memory
**Imagery:** Landscape as moral weather; war/violence as physical texture; aftermath objects
**Dialogue:** Sparse and loaded; silence and avoidance do the work
**POV:** Close third with occasional widening to collective witness
**Pacing:** Nonlinear or braided; short chapters; recurring motifs with altered meaning
**Themes:** War, violence, survival, displacement, grief, endurance, history's cost
**Signature tells:** Image-led transitions replace explanatory bridges; aftermath outweighs the battle; motifs recur as memory architecture
**Avoid:** Gratuitous suffering without interior consequence; explaining the moral
**Knobs:** Brutality (implied→explicit) · Lyric intensity (spare→incantatory) · Timeline (linear→braided)

### XS08 — Elegiac Social Intimacy
**Voice/Tone:** Elegant, reflective, precise; quiet ache; restraint amplifying impact
**Diction:** Controlled and exact; nuance in social markers, manners, interior naming
**Syntax:** Measured cadence; long reflective sentences balanced by crisp scene beats
**Imagery:** Houses, landscapes, seasons; social spaces as emotional stages
**Dialogue:** Subtext-heavy; polite surfaces with private detonations underneath
**POV:** Close but slightly distanced; memory may be unreliable without overt signals
**Pacing:** Slow reveal; retrospective pivots; the real plot arrives late and feels inevitable
**Themes:** Family, desire, regret, class, secrecy, faith, self-deception
**Signature tells:** Calm scene ends with a sentence that changes everything; etiquette doubles as emotional violence; narrator misremembers, corrects too late
**Avoid:** Ambient melancholy without movement; over-explaining subtext
**Knobs:** Reliability (clear→subtly unreliable) · Heat (cool→acute longing) · Pace (languid→quietly tense)

### XS09 — Moral Pressure Dark
**Voice/Tone:** Austere, incisive, relentless; dread and ethical confrontation; bleak humor never comforting
**Diction:** Stripped clarity; biblical/ritual phrasing or clinical coldness as blade
**Syntax:** Pared sentences; controlled repetition; incantatory and punishing when long
**Imagery:** Institutions, barren spaces, bodies under coercion; symbols that feel physical
**Dialogue:** Sparse, power-dynamic; threats often implicit
**POV:** Close or coolly observant; intimacy increases claustrophobia
**Pacing:** Escalating ordeal; suspense as moral inquiry; endings refusing easy relief
**Themes:** Power, violence, complicity, civilization, coercion, survival, guilt
**Signature tells:** Moral choice with no clean option; violence described simply, then aftermath lingers; recurring symbols as ethical spine
**Avoid:** Nihilism without a question; shock for shock's sake
**Knobs:** Hope (none→ember) · Allegory (thin→explicit) · Suspense (psychological→plot-driven)

### XS10 — Mythic Parable Wonder
**Voice/Tone:** Oracular, curious, humanistic; matter-of-fact about the impossible; calm philosophical smile
**Diction:** Plain-elevated; fable clarity; occasional aphoristic turns
**Syntax:** Omniscient sweep; sentences stretch like folklore, then snap to clean fact
**Imagery:** Miracles as ordinary; animals, weather, objects as recurring emblems
**Dialogue:** Spare, archetypal; conversation as moral lever
**POV:** Panoramic omniscience; time telescopes without apology
**Pacing:** Episodic; circular motifs; parable logic (events rhyme more than cause)
**Themes:** Faith, fate, institutions, love, migration, history, absurdity of authority
**Signature tells:** A miracle with no fanfare; symbolic recurrence replaces explanation; ending reframes tale as question
**Avoid:** Over-explaining magic; moralizing replacing story movement
**Knobs:** Surreal level (light shimmer→full myth) · Humor (none→sly) · Moral explicitness (implicit→aphoristic)

### XS11 — Haunted Mythic Lyric
**Voice/Tone:** Visionary, myth-soaked, polyphonic; spiritual intensity; grief and celebration braided
**Diction:** Lush and musical; ritual language alongside local idiom; repetition as power
**Syntax:** Chant-like rhythms; long musical runs plus sharp drumbeat fragments
**Imagery:** Ancestors, rivers, ghosts, trees, thresholds; myth overlays everyday
**Dialogue:** Choral; overlapping voices; speech as spell and testimony
**POV:** Multiple and shifting; first person plural can appear; perspective slides like dream logic
**Pacing:** Braided, nonlinear; refrains; stories-within-stories; emotional crescendos
**Themes:** Ancestry, diaspora, identity, trauma, redemption, community, sacred/ordinary
**Signature tells:** Refrains repeat with accumulating meaning; mythic imagery punctuates realism like lightning; narrative voice becomes communal ritual
**Avoid:** Polyphony without emotional throughline; explaining myth
**Knobs:** Polyphony (single voice→chorus) · Myth density (hints→full cosmology) · Clarity (guided→dreamlike)

### XS12 — Architectural Skepticism
**Voice/Tone:** Cool, essayistic, precise; ironic restraint; empathy via accuracy not sentiment
**Diction:** Clean sentences; archival/financial/legal vocabulary with deliberate clarity
**Syntax:** Controlled and legible; structural turns do heavy lifting
**Imagery:** Documents, ledgers, buildings, cities, media artifacts; money as atmosphere
**Dialogue:** Minimal and targeted; reveals through competing accounts
